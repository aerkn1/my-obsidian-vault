## 2ï¸âƒ£ Quota Sampling

### Definition
Non-random selection to meet **predetermined quotas** for subgroups

### Process
1. Determine desired subgroup representation
2. Set quotas for each subgroup
3. Recruit until quotas filled
4. **No random selection** within quotas

### Example
Survey shoppers to represent population:
- Need 200 total
- Quota: 100 men, 100 women
- Quota: 50 age 18-30, 50 age 31-50, 50 age 51-70, 50 age 70+
- Recruit at mall until all quotas filled

### Advantages
âœ… Ensures representation
âœ… More practical than stratified random
âœ… Can control important characteristics
âœ… Quick

### Disadvantages
âŒ Selection bias within quotas
âŒ Interviewer chooses who to approach
âŒ Can't calculate sampling error
âŒ May not be representative

### Quota vs. Stratified
Both divide into subgroups, BUT:
- **Quota**: Non-random selection within groups
- **Stratified**: Random selection within groups

## 3ï¸âƒ£ Purposive/Judgment Sampling

### Definition
Researcher **deliberately selects** "information-rich" cases

### Types

**A. Typical Case Sampling**
Select "average" or "representative" cases

**Example**: Study typical startup
- Not extreme success or failure
- Medium-sized team
- Common industry

**B. Extreme/Deviant Case Sampling**
Select unusual or outlier cases

**Example**: Study extreme startup success
- Unicorn companies
- Understand what makes them special

**C. Critical Case Sampling**
"If it works here, it will work anywhere"

**Example**: Test training program
- At worst-performing location
- If it works there, should work everywhere

**D. Homogeneous Sampling**
Select similar cases for focused analysis

**Example**: Study only female CEOs in tech
- Reduce variation
- Focus on specific experience

**E. Maximum Variation Sampling**
Select diverse cases to capture range

**Example**: Study organizational culture
- Small startups, large corporations
- Tech, manufacturing, service sectors
- Capture full spectrum

### Advantages
âœ… Gets relevant information
âœ… Efficient with limited resources
âœ… Good for qualitative research
âœ… Focuses on information-rich cases

### Disadvantages
âŒ Researcher bias
âŒ No statistical generalization
âŒ Subjective selection
âŒ Unknown representativeness

## 5ï¸âƒ£ Theoretical Sampling

### Definition
Sampling driven by **emerging theory** (grounded theory approach)

### Process
1. Start with initial purposive sample
2. Collect and analyze data
3. Theory begins to emerge
4. Sample additional cases to test/refine theory
5. Continue until **theoretical saturation**

### Theoretical Saturation
**Stop when**: New data adds no new insights

### Example
Study of entrepreneurial failure:

**Round 1** (n=5):
- Interview failed founders
- Pattern: Funding issues mentioned

**Round 2** (n=5):
- Purposely sample cases with funding problems
- Confirm pattern, new insight: timing matters

**Round 3** (n=5):
- Sample failed ventures at different times
- Theory: Funding + market timing interaction

**Round 4** (n=3):
- No new insights â†’ Stop (saturation reached)

### Advantages
âœ… Theory-driven, responsive
âœ… Efficient for theory building
âœ… Flexible
âœ… Deep theoretical insights

### Disadvantages
